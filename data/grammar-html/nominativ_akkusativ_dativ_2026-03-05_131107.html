📖 Explanation

What is it?

German uses a system of grammatical cases to show the role each noun plays in a sentence. The case of a noun determines the form of its article — this is why der/die/das change. At A1 level, you need three cases: Nominativ (the subject), Akkusativ (the direct object), and Dativ (the indirect object or following certain prepositions).

When to use each case

NOMINATIV — The subject: the person or thing performing the action.
Ask: "Who or what is doing this?"
Example: Der Mann schläft. (The man is sleeping.)

AKKUSATIV — The direct object: what is directly affected by the action.
Ask: "Who or what is being acted on?"
Example: Ich kaufe den Kaffee. (I buy the coffee.)

DATIV — The indirect object: to whom or for whom something is done. Also required by certain prepositions when expressing location.
Ask: "To whom? For whom? Where is it (with a location preposition)?"
Example: Ich gebe dem Mann das Buch. (I give the man the book.)

How to form it — Definite Articles (der/die/das)

Dativdemderdemden

What changes (red = changed from Nominativ):

  • Masculine changes in BOTH Akkusativ and Dativ: der → den → dem
  • Feminine changes only in Dativ: die → der
  • Neuter changes only in Dativ: das → dem
  • Plural changes only in Dativ: die → den

Memory trick: In Akkusativ, ONLY masculine changes (der → den). In Dativ, ALL genders change: masculine and neuter both become dem, feminine becomes der.

Note: There is no plural of ein — for plural indefinite nouns simply use no article: Ich kaufe Äpfel.

  • Masculine Akkusativ: ein → einen
  • All genders in Dativ gain a suffix: -em (m), -er (f), -em (n)

How to form it — Negative Article (kein/keine)

kein/keine means "not a / no" and follows the exact same pattern as ein/eine, plus it has a plural form:

CaseMasculine (m)Feminine (f)Neuter (n)Plural
Nominativkeinkeinekeinkeine
Akkusativkeinenkeinekeinkeine
Dativkeinemkeinerkeinemkeinen

Usage: Use kein to negate nouns that take ein or no article.

  • Ich habe ein Auto. → Ich habe kein Auto.
  • Ich habe Hunger. → Ich habe keinen Hunger. (masculine Akkusativ!)

⚠️ Common mistake: Do NOT use nicht ein to negate a noun — use kein instead.

Dative Prepositions — The Connection You Noticed

Your observation is exactly right. When a preposition forces dative case, the article changes to its dative form — it is the same case system at work.

ALWAYS DATIVE (at A1): mit, bei, zu, von, aus, nach, seit, gegenüber

TWO-WAY PREPOSITIONS (the ones you asked about):
an, auf, hinter, in, neben, über, unter, vor, zwischen

  • Use DATIV when answering "Where is it?" (location)
  • Use AKKUSATIV when answering "Where is it going?" (direction/movement)
QuestionCaseExample
Where IS it? (location)DativDas Restaurant ist neben dem Hotel.
Where is it GOING? (direction)AkkusativIch gehe neben das Hotel.

After dative prepositions, the articles become:

  • Masculine → dem (der → dem)
  • Feminine → der (die → der)
  • Neuter → dem (das → dem)
  • Plural → den (die → den)

Common contractions (you will see these constantly):

  • in + dem = im → Ich bin im Hotel.
  • an + dem = am → Er steht am Bahnhof.
  • zu + dem = zum → Ich gehe zum Restaurant.
  • zu + der = zur → Ich gehe zur Apotheke.

Common Exceptions

  1. Plural nouns in Dativ add -n to the noun itself (if the plural doesn't already end in -n or -s):
    die Männer → den Männern  |  die Kinder → den Kindern  |  die Hotels → den Hotels
  2. N-nouns (masculine nouns that add -en in all cases except Nominativ):
    der Herr → den Herrn (Akk) → dem Herrn (Dat)
    Not very common at A1, but worth recognizing.

🌍 Transfer Notes

  • Spanish has gender (el/la) but only 2 genders. German has 3 (m/f/n) and the article changes with case — don't map Spanish el/la directly to German der/die/das.
  • Spanish uses indirect objects with clitic pronouns (le doy el libro a él), which parallels Dativ closely. The concept is the same; the mechanism differs.
  • English has no article changes for case at all — 'the' and 'a' never change. This makes case invisible to English speakers; you must consciously track each noun's role.
  • The Spanish 'personal a' (marking a direct-object person: veo a Juan) has no German equivalent — German uses Akkusativ article forms (den/einen/keinen) to signal the direct object.
🔑 Key Takeaway: In Akkusativ only the masculine article changes (der → den, ein → einen, kein → keinen); in Dativ ALL genders change — and the same changes apply whether the dative comes from an indirect object or from a dative preposition like vor, neben, or an.

✏️ Examples

Simple 3 examples — Nominativ / Akkusativ basics

1. Der Zug ist schnell.
→ The train is fast.
Nominativ masculine: 'der' — Zug is the subject performing no action but being described.
2. Ich kaufe einen Kaffee.
→ I am buying a coffee.
Akkusativ masculine indefinite: ein → einen — Kaffee is the direct object of kaufen.
3. Das ist kein Hotel.
→ That is not a hotel.
Nominativ neuter: 'kein' — Hotel is neuter (das Hotel); kein does not change in Nominativ neuter.

Medium 3 examples — Akkusativ + Dativ prepositions

4. Ich sehe den Bahnhof nicht.
→ I don't see the train station.
Akkusativ masculine definite: der → den — Bahnhof is the direct object of sehen.
5. Sie hat keinen Hunger.
→ She is not hungry. (lit. She has no hunger.)
Akkusativ masculine: kein → keinen — Hunger is masculine (der Hunger) and the direct object of haben.
6. Das Café ist neben dem Hotel.
→ The café is next to the hotel.
Dativ neuter after 'neben' (location): das → dem — Hotel is neuter and the location is fixed (not a direction).

Complex 6 examples — Double objects, multiple prepositions, Perfekt

7. Ich gebe dem Kellner ein Trinkgeld.
→ I give the waiter a tip.
Double object: 'dem Kellner' = Dativ masculine (indirect object, der → dem); 'ein Trinkgeld' = Akkusativ neuter indefinite (no change for neuter).
8. Das Restaurant ist neben der Apotheke und vor dem Bahnhof.
→ The restaurant is next to the pharmacy and in front of the train station.
Two location prepositions, both forcing Dativ: 'neben der Apotheke' (feminine die → der) and 'vor dem Bahnhof' (masculine der → dem).
9. Er hat kein Ticket gekauft, weil er kein Geld hat.
→ He didn't buy a ticket because he has no money.
Akkusativ neuter: 'kein Ticket' and 'kein Geld' — both neuter nouns, Akkusativ of kein = kein (no change for neuter).
10. Ich wohne in einem Hotel in der Stadt.
→ I live in a hotel in the city.
Dativ after 'in' (location): 'einem Hotel' (neuter indefinite: ein → einem) and 'der Stadt' (feminine definite: die → der).
11. Sie sucht eine Apotheke, aber sie findet keine.
→ She is looking for a pharmacy, but she doesn't find one.
Akkusativ feminine: 'eine Apotheke' and 'keine' — feminine indefinite Akkusativ stays eine/keine (no change for feminine).
12. Er hat dem Taxifahrer den Bahnhof gezeigt.
→ He showed the taxi driver the train station.
Perfekt with double object: 'dem Taxifahrer' (Dativ masculine indirect object) + 'den Bahnhof' (Akkusativ masculine direct object).

2. Fill in blank · Simple
→ ein

3. Fill in blank · Medium

4. Fill in blank · Medium

5. Fill in blank · Complex
→ dem, ein
'Dem Kellner' = Dativ masculine definite (indirect object). 'Ein Trinkgeld' = Akkusativ neuter indefinite (neuter has no change in Akkusativ).
6. Multiple choice · Simple
→ a) ein
Hotel is neuter (das Hotel). Nominativ neuter indefinite = 'ein'. 'Einen' is masculine Akkusativ; 'einem' is Dativ.
7. Multiple choice · Simple
→ c) eine
Apotheke is feminine (die Apotheke). Nominativ feminine indefinite = 'eine'. 'Ein' is masculine/neuter; 'einen' is masculine Akkusativ.
8. Multiple choice · Medium
→ b) den
Mann is the direct object (Akkusativ), masculine. Masculine definite Akkusativ = 'den'. 'Der' is Nominativ; 'dem' is Dativ.
9. Multiple choice · Complex
→ c) dem
'Auf' for location forces Dativ. Tisch is masculine (der Tisch), so Dativ masculine definite = 'dem'. Direction (putting the book onto the table) would be Akkusativ: 'den Tisch'.

→ Die Frau hat kein Auto.
'Frau' is Nominativ subject (die Frau). 'Auto' is neuter (das Auto), Akkusativ. Neuter Akkusativ of kein = 'kein' (no change).
11. Translation · Medium
→ Ich suche den Bahnhof.
'Bahnhof' is masculine (der Bahnhof), Akkusativ direct object of suchen. Masculine definite Akkusativ = 'den'.
12. Translation · Complex
→ Das Restaurant ist neben der Apotheke.
'Neben' for location forces Dativ. Apotheke is feminine (die Apotheke). Feminine definite Dativ = 'der'.
13. Translation · Complex
→ Er hat kein Ticket gekauft.
'Ticket' is neuter (das Ticket), Akkusativ. Neuter Akkusativ of kein = 'kein' (no change). Perfekt: hat + past participle at the end.
14. Error correction · Simple
→ Ich kaufe den Kaffee.
'Kaffee' is masculine Akkusativ direct object. 'Der' (Nominativ) must change to 'den' (Akkusativ masculine).
15. Error correction · Medium
→ Das Hotel ist neben der Apotheke.
'Neben' for location forces Dativ. Apotheke is feminine. Feminine definite Dativ = 'der', not 'die' (which is Nominativ/Akkusativ).
16. Error correction · Complex
→ Ich habe keinen Hunger, aber ich esse ein Sandwich.
'Hunger' is masculine (der Hunger), Akkusativ direct object of haben. Kein → keinen in Akkusativ masculine. The second clause is already correct: Sandwich is neuter, neuter Akkusativ of ein = 'ein'.
